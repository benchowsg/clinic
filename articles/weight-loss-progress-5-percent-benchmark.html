By Dr. Gerald Thang

Principal Physician

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), Graduate Diploma in Cardiology

Losing 5% of your body weight in the first three months is actually considered a successful "clinical response" by most medical standards, though it often feels slow compared to the dramatic results seen on social media.

In clinical trials for prescription weight management treatment, losing 5% or more within the first 12–16 weeks is the benchmark used to determine if a patient is a "responder." Here is a breakdown of what that means for you and the factors you should consider before deciding whether to continue.

1. You are likely still in the "Titration Phase"

Most prescription weight management treatments start at a very low dose to help your body adjust and minimize side effects (nausea, etc.).

  • The "Starter" Dose: The doses you take in months 1 and 2 are often not meant for significant weight loss; they are meant to prep your system.
  • The "Therapeutic" Dose: Significant weight loss typically accelerates once you reach the higher maintenance doses. If you haven’t reached the maximum dose yet, you haven't seen the treatment's full potential.

2. The Role of Increased Exercise

It is great that you have increased your exercise, but keep two things in mind:

  • Muscle vs. Fat: If you are strength training, you may be gaining muscle while losing fat. Muscle is denser than fat, so the scale might not move as much even though your body composition is improving.
  • The "Scale Mask": Increased exercise can sometimes cause temporary water retention as muscles repair themselves.

3. What "5% in 3 Months" Means

Percent Lost Clinical Interpretation
Less than 5% Often labeled an "under-responder"; doctors may suggest switching medications.
5% to 10% A solid, healthy start. This is a sustainable rate that reduces the risk of skin sagging and muscle wasting.
Over 10% Rapid loss; requires close monitoring to ensure nutritional needs are met.

4. Factors to Discuss with Your Doctor

Before deciding to stop, consider these common reasons for a slower start:

  • Metabolic Conditions: Conditions like PCOS, insulin resistance, or hypothyroidism can slow the initial response.
  • Protein Intake: If you aren't eating enough protein while exercising, your metabolism might slow down to protect muscle tissue.
  • Dose Adjustment: Your doctor might decide to accelerate your dose increase if your side effects are manageable.
  • Nutritional Density: Quality of intake remains critical for metabolic health even when appetite is suppressed.
  • Managing Satiety: Patterns of satiety may fluctuate until a steady state is achieved in the body.
  • The Path Forward: If progress targets aren't met at maximum dosage, we pivot to a broader metabolic audit.

Should you continue?

Generally, if you have reached 5% loss and aren't experiencing debilitating side effects, most doctors would encourage you to stay the course until you reach the maximum dose for at least a month before calling it a "fail."

The big question for your next check-up: "Am I on the therapeutic dose yet, or are we still titrating up?"
