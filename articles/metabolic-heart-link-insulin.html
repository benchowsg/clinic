Your heart is already in danger, and it's probably not what you think.

By Dr. Gerald Thang

Principal Physician

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), Graduate Diploma in Cardiology

You go for your annual health screening. The numbers come back and your cholesterol borderline, fasting glucose normal, blood pressure "a bit high but manageable." Your doctor says to watch your diet. You leave feeling mostly reassured.

But here is what that screening likely missed: the real reason Singaporeans are dying of heart disease decades too early. It is not just high cholesterol. It is insulin resistance, a condition that silently inflames your arteries for years before any standard test flags it. And by the time it does show up, the damage has already begun.

These are not abstract statistics. They are your colleagues, your parents, your neighbours, and quite possibly, you. The most dangerous part? Most people in this group feel completely fine right now.

Why this matters more than you think

Think about what you want your life to look like at 65, or 75. Walking your grandchildren to school. Travelling with your spouse. Getting up from a chair without help. Playing golf on weekends. Not spending your retirement years managing dialysis appointments, recovering from a stroke, or learning to walk again after an amputation.

That future is not guaranteed — but it is within your control. The choices made in your 40s and 50s determine the quality of life in your 70s and beyond. The gap between a vibrant, independent old age and a dependent, medically-managed one is not luck. It is biology — and biology can be changed.

The cost of waiting

In Singapore, diabetes is a leading cause of kidney failure, heart disease, stroke, and vision loss. Once these complications set in, they are largely irreversible. Kidney dialysis runs three sessions a week, every week, for life. A stroke can take away your speech, your movement, your independence — often permanently.

But here is what the research consistently shows: the earlier you identify and address insulin resistance and metabolic strain, the more completely it can be reversed. This is not about treating disease. It is about reclaiming decades.

1. The problem: the "TOFI" trap

In Singapore, many of us are TOFI — Thin on the Outside, Fat on the Inside. You may look fine at the office, wear a healthy BMI, pass your IPPT. But internally, visceral fat is accumulating around your liver, heart, and abdominal organs — invisible on the outside, dangerous on the inside.

Asians develop metabolic problems at a lower body weight than Caucasians. Our bodies are genetically less tolerant of visceral fat — meaning the same amount of hidden fat causes far more metabolic harm in a Chinese, Malay, or Indian person than in a Caucasian at the same BMI. This is not a theory. It is why Singapore has one of the highest diabetes rates in the developed world despite not being an obese nation.

2. The why: insulin resistance and your heart

Insulin is the key that unlocks your cells so glucose can enter and be used as energy. When you become insulin resistant, those locks stop working properly. Your pancreas compensates by producing more and more insulin to force the doors open.

For years — sometimes a decade or more — your blood sugar stays "normal." Your fasting glucose looks fine. Your doctor sees nothing alarming. But the whole time, chronically elevated insulin is inflaming the inner walls of your arteries, raising your triglycerides, lowering your protective HDL cholesterol, and accelerating the build-up of arterial plaque. The plaques that rupture are the ones that cause heart attacks.

By the time fasting glucose finally flags, you have likely had insulin resistance for 10 years already. The window for easy, reversible intervention has long closed.

The hawker centre factor

A typical local lunch — chicken rice, teh tarik, and a cut of fruit — can deliver 80 to 100 grams of refined carbohydrate in under 30 minutes. That triggers a sustained insulin spike. Repeated daily, over years, it gradually trains your cells into resistance.

This is not about blaming our food culture. It is about understanding that the food environment most Singaporeans live in creates a constant metabolic load — and that load accumulates silently, long before any symptom appears.

3. Why early action gives you more good years

The goal is not just to avoid dying. It is to stay well — to remain mobile, sharp, and independent for as long as possible. Singapore's population is ageing rapidly. As of 2026, more than 1 in 5 Singaporeans is aged 65 or above. The question is not whether you will get older. It is whether you will age with your health intact.

Insulin resistance, caught early, is fully reversible through lifestyle change. Arterial inflammation, identified early, can be brought down. Early metabolic intervention has been shown to reduce the risk of heart attack, stroke, and diabetes — the three conditions most likely to rob you of your independence in later life.

Prevention is not just for the sick

Most people wait for a diagnosis before taking their health seriously. But prevention works precisely because you act before the damage is done. Waiting for symptoms is waiting for a problem that, by definition, has already progressed beyond the easy-to-reverse stage. The Singaporeans who stay healthy into their 70s and 80s are not lucky — they are the ones who started paying attention in their 40s and 50s.

4. Beyond the basics: what to actually check

Standard corporate screenings measure fasting blood glucose — one of the last markers to turn abnormal. To understand your true metabolic and cardiovascular risk, you need markers that reveal what is happening years earlier.

  • Fasting insulin and HOMA-IR: Measures your insulin sensitivity directly — not just your blood sugar. This is the earliest signal of insulin resistance.
  • HbA1c: A 3-month average of blood sugar trends, far more informative than a single fasting glucose reading.
  • Full lipid panel with triglyceride ratio: High triglycerides and low HDL are the metabolic fingerprint of insulin resistance, and often appear years before glucose rises.
  • hsCRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein): Detects low-grade arterial inflammation — the hidden driver of plaque build-up.
  • Waist circumference: Not BMI. Waist measurement reflects visceral fat far more accurately, especially for Asians.
  • Blood pressure (both arms): A simple test that reveals far more about arterial health than most people realise.

MOH recommends Singaporeans aged 40 and above screen for diabetes, hypertension, and high cholesterol regularly. Many doctors advise Asians with a family history to start at 35. Under Healthier SG, eligible Singapore Citizens can access cardiovascular risk screening at $0 at their enrolled clinic or polyclinic.

5. What restoration looks like

The good news: insulin resistance is one of the most reversible conditions in medicine. Studies show meaningful improvement in insulin sensitivity within weeks of sustained lifestyle change — not years. The prescription is not exotic.

Reduce the refined carbohydrate load — not eliminate carbs, but choose brown rice over white, cut sugary drinks including fruit juice and bubble tea, and eat protein and vegetables before starch to blunt the glucose spike. Walk after meals — even 10 minutes significantly lowers post-meal blood sugar. Prioritise sleep — chronic sleep deprivation drives insulin resistance as powerfully as diet. Build muscle — resistance training is one of the most effective tools available, because muscle is the body's primary glucose sink.
