7 Things You Should Know About Heart Failure

By Dr. Gerald Thang

Principal Physician

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), Graduate Diploma in Cardiology (Singapore)

Heart failure can happen to anyone. In the real world, heart failure isn't about the heart "stopping", it's about the heart losing its ability to pump with the efficiency required to sustain your lifestyle.

1. Recognizing the Symptoms of Pump Failure

When the heart engine weakens, fluid begins to accumulate in the body (edema):

  • Shortness of Breath: Especially when lying flat at night.
  • Peripheral Edema: Noticeable swelling in the legs or ankles.
  • Reduced Stamina: A feeling that your "internal battery" is draining faster.

2. The Singapore Context: NYHA vs. ACC/AHA Stages

In Singapore, cardiologists use two different scales to track your health. While NYHA tracks your symptoms, the ACC/AHA Stages track the structural damage to your heart.

Structural Stages (A-D)

Stage A: At risk (High blood pressure/Diabetes) but no damage yet.

Stage B: Structural damage exists, but no symptoms felt yet.

Stage C: Current or previous symptoms of heart failure.

Stage D: Advanced heart failure requiring specialized intervention.

3. The Functional Classes (NYHA)

This scale determines how much your condition limits your daily lifestyle:

Class I: No limitation. Ordinary activity causes no fatigue.

Class II: Slight limitation. Comfortable at rest, but ordinary activity results in fatigue.

Class III: Marked limitation. Comfortable only at rest.

Class IV: Symptoms present even at rest.

4. It’s Not Always Due to a Heart Attack

Many patients develop heart failure through "silent" stressors like uncontrolled hypertension, obstructive sleep apnea, or irregular heart rates.

5. Acute vs. Chronic Heart Failure

Acute Heart Failure is a sudden complication (often an emergency). It's often from a heart attack or severe infection. Chronic Heart Failure is the long-term result of conditions like kidney failure or chronic lung disease. Both require an "intermediate care" strategy to manage effectively.

6. Modern Reversal Strategies

Treatment in 2026 focuses on Metabolic Synergy to strengthen the muscle, such as ARNI and SGLT2 Inhibitors. These are modern therapy providing vascular protection and increases survival rate.

7. Detection Even Without Symptoms

Asymptomatic heart failure (Stage B) can be detected via Echocardiogram, CT, or MRI Heart. Early detection in Stage B is the best way to prevent progressing to Stage C.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is heart failure a death sentence?

Absolutely not. In 2026, it is a manageable chronic condition. Modern agents can significantly improve heart function and quality of life.
