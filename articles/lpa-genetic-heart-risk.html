The Rise of Lp(a): Uncovering the Hidden Genetic Risk Factor

Written & Reviewed by Dr. Gerald Thang

Principal Physician

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), Graduate Diploma in Cardiology (Singapore)

In modern cardiology, we frequently encounter patients who lead exemplary lifestyles, maintaining a balanced diet and regular exercise, yet still present with unexpected coronary artery disease. Often, the missing piece of this clinical puzzle is Lipoprotein(a), or Lp(a). This is a genetically determined particle that, unlike standard LDL cholesterol, remains largely unaffected by traditional lifestyle changes or standard statin therapies. It is now recognized as a potent, independent risk factor for heart attacks and strokes, particularly in younger individuals.

The danger of Lp(a) lies in its dual threat nature: it is both highly pro-atherogenic (promoting plaque buildup) and pro-thrombotic (increasing the risk of blood clots). Because its levels are determined by your DNA, many patients remain unaware of their risk until a cardiovascular event occurs. This makes a single lifetime screening for Lp(a) an essential component of any comprehensive cardiac evaluation, especially for those with a family history of early heart disease.

As the Principal Physician, Dr. Gerald Thang identifies Lp(a) as a critical "red flag" that necessitates a shift in clinical strategy. When high Lp(a) is detected, the objective is no longer just standard prevention. The role of the Principal Physician is to implement a more aggressive risk reduction framework, often lowering other modifiable targets like LDL-C even further to offset the inherent genetic risk posed by Lp(a). This is precision medicine in practice: interpreting genetic data to prevent clinical events before they manifest.

Managing high Lp(a) requires a nuanced approach. While traditional medications have limited impact on the Lp(a) particle itself, new generation therapies are currently in development that specifically target its production. Until these are widely available, the focus remains on Total Risk Optimization. This involves using advanced imaging, such as a CT Coronary Angiogram, to determine if the elevated genetic marker has already begun to manifest as physical plaque, allowing for a proactive rather than reactive treatment plan.

Ultimately, the rise of Lp(a) testing represents a shift toward a more individualized understanding of heart health. By uncovering these hidden genetic factors, we move away from population based averages and toward a strategy that respects each patient's unique biological blueprint. Identifying this "silent" risk factor early is one of the most effective ways to rewrite a patient's cardiovascular future.
