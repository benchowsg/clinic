7 Things You Should Know About Heart Failure

By Dr. Gerald Thang

Principal Physician

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), Graduate Diploma in Cardiology (Singapore)

Heart failure can happen to anyone, at any time. In the real world, heart failure isn't about the heart "stopping"—it's about the heart losing its ability to pump with the efficiency required to sustain your lifestyle. Understanding the nuances of this condition is the first step toward reclaiming your vitality.

1. Recognizing the Symptoms of Pump Failure

The heart acts as the body's engine. When that engine weakens, fluid begins to accumulate in the body (edema). This manifests in ways that are often mistaken for general fatigue:

  • Shortness of Breath: Especially when performing routine tasks or lying flat at night.
  • Peripheral Edema: Noticeable swelling in the legs, ankles, or abdomen.
  • Reduced Stamina: A feeling that your "internal battery" is draining faster than usual.

2. It’s Not Always Due to a Heart Attack

While a heart attack is a common cause, many patients develop heart failure through "silent" stressors. Long-term injury from uncontrolled hypertension, obstructive sleep apnea, or irregular heart rates (arrhythmias) can gradually degrade the heart's pumping power.

3. The Four Stages of Heart Failure (NYHA)

We use the New York Heart Association (NYHA) classification to determine the level of clinical intervention needed:

Class I: No limitation of physical activity. Ordinary activity does not cause undue fatigue.

Class II: Slight limitation. Comfortable at rest, but ordinary physical activity results in fatigue or palpitations.

Class III: Marked limitation. Comfortable at rest, but less than ordinary activity causes symptoms.

Class IV: Unable to carry out any physical activity without discomfort. Symptoms present even at rest.

4. Acute vs. Chronic Heart Failure

Heart failure is classified by its onset. Acute Heart Failure is a sudden complication—often from a heart attack or severe infection. Chronic Heart Failure is the long-term result of existing conditions like kidney failure or chronic lung disease. Both require a specialized "Intermediate Care" strategy to manage effectively.

5. The Triggers of Worsening (Exacerbation)

Stability in heart failure is fragile. Common triggers that can cause a "crash" include:

  • Fluid Indiscretion: Consuming more fluid than the heart can process.
  • Medication Non-compliance: Missing doses or incorrect timing.
  • New Infections: Such as pneumonia, which places extreme stress on the cardiovascular system.

6. Modern Reversal & Prevention Strategies

Treatment is no longer just about managing water with diuretics. We utilize Metabolic Synergy to strengthen the heart:

ARNIs (e.g., Entresto): A modern class of drugs that increases survival and reduces hospitalizations.
Beta-Blockers: Controls heart rate to allow the heart to pump more efficiently.
SGLT2 Inhibitors: A cornerstone of 2026 heart failure therapy that provides significant vascular protection.

7. Detection Even Without Symptoms

You do not have to wait for shortness of breath to take action. Asymptomatic heart failure can be detected through specialized imaging:

  • Echocardiogram: Real-time ultrasound of heart muscle contractions.
  • Treadmill Stress Test: Assessing the functional capacity of your body under load.
  • MRI Heart: Providing clear insight into the structure of the muscle to catch structural disease early.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is heart failure a death sentence?

Absolutely not. In 2026, heart failure is a manageable chronic condition. With early detection and modern pharmacological agents like ARNIs and SGLT2 inhibitors, we can significantly improve heart function, enhance quality of life, and in many cases, reverse the progression of the disease.

Can I exercise if I have heart failure?

Yes, and it is highly recommended. However, it must be done through "Cardiac Reconditioning"—a supervised approach to exercise that gradually builds heart strength without causing acute stress. We recommend a clinical review before starting any new fitness routine.

What is the difference between heart failure and a heart attack?

A heart attack is a "plumbing" issue where an artery is blocked, causing muscle death. Heart failure is a "mechanical" issue where the heart muscle is too weak or too stiff to pump blood effectively. While a heart attack can cause heart failure, they are distinct clinical conditions.

Why do my legs swell with heart failure?

When the heart pump weakens, blood "backs up" in the veins. This pressure causes fluid to leak into the surrounding tissues, typically settling in the lowest points of the body like the ankles and legs due to gravity. This is known as peripheral edema.
