You’re Doing Everything Right. So Why Isn’t Your Cholesterol Improving?

By Dr. Gerald Thang

Principal Physician

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), Graduate Diploma in Cardiology

You’ve heard the advice. Eat more fibre, less salt, cut the sugar, no carbs, exercise regularly, go easy on the alcohol. Maybe you’ve even followed it genuinely, consistently, for months.

And yet your numbers haven’t moved the way you expected. Or they improved for a while and then plateaued. Or you checked the range and it says they’re “acceptable” but you’re not convinced that acceptable is good enough.

You’re not imagining it. And you’re not failing. For a significant portion of people, lifestyle changes alone have a ceiling. By understanding why that ceiling exists is more useful than simply trying harder.

What Lifestyle Changes Actually Do

To be clear: diet, exercise, and weight management are genuinely effective and should always be the foundation. They are not optional extras. But they work primarily by reducing the inputs: less dietary fat, less sugar converting to triglycerides, and less inflammatory load from excess weight.

What lifestyle cannot do is change the underlying biology that determines how your body produces, processes, and clears cholesterol in the first place. That biology varies enormously from person to person.

When the Problem Is Upstream

Think of your blood cholesterol like a swimming pool. The cholesterol level is controlled by two things: what your liver produces, and how fast your body clears it.

The Reality: No amount of oats or olive oil can fully compensate for a liver that doesn't turn down the cholesterol production, or a system that doesn't clear cholesterol fast enough. These are physiological realities, not failures of willpower.

What Standard Blood Test Panels Can Miss

A standard cholesterol panel measures LDL cholesterol by concentration. What it doesn’t tell you is how many particles (ApoB) are actually circulating and particle count, not concentration alone, is what determines how much arterial damage is accumulating over time.

Once you understand that two people can have the same LDL reading but very different levels of cardiovascular risk, you will realise why some patients with “normal” cholesterol still go on to have cardiac events. Without looking deeper, you cannot know which category you are in.

Signs That Lifestyle Changes Have Reached Their Limit
  • Your LDL or triglycerides remain elevated after 3 to 6 months of genuine effort.
  • Your numbers improved initially and then stopped responding further.
  • You have a family history of early heart disease, regardless of your current readings.
  • Your results are “borderline” and you’ve been told to “watch and wait” without a clear plan.
  • You feel well and results look “acceptable,” but you want to know your actual risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is high cholesterol always genetic?

Not always, but genetics set the 'baseline.' While lifestyle can lower your numbers, your genetic makeup determines how much of a decrease you can achieve through diet and exercise alone.

What is a 'Lipid Audit' compared to a regular test?

A standard test looks at total concentration. A Lipid Audit looks at particle count (ApoB), inflammation markers, and sometimes imaging to see if those particles are actually causing plaque buildup.
